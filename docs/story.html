Where Toronto needs bike lanes

Mined from 7.8 million Bike Share Toronto rides, this map shows routes where cyclists ride far more than geography alone predicts — and where the city hasn't yet built a protected lane to support them.

1. The desire lines

A simple gravity model says trips between two stations should scale with the product of their dock capacities divided by the square of their distance (fitted α = 1.59 on 2025 data). Pairs where the actual ride count wildly exceeds that baseline are the city's desire lines — routes pulling cyclists disproportionately. Most of the top desire lines trace Toronto's existing protected infrastructure: the Martin Goodman Trail, the Bloor bike lane, the Adelaide bike lane, the Don Valley trail, King Street West.

2. The infrastructure gap

Now flip the question: which high-affinity routes have no protected infrastructure? Filtering to pairs with ≥400 trips/year, top-25% affinity, and less than 20% of the straight-line path within 50m of a protected bike lane or cycle track returns 50 candidate gaps. They cluster overwhelmingly in Toronto's West End — Mimico, Humber Bay, Parkdale, Roncesvalles, Liberty Village, High Park, and the Bathurst corridor up to St. Clair. These are routes cyclists are riding heavily without a safe lane to do it on.

3. Do the City's plans already cover the gap?

The obvious next question: is the City already planning to fix these routes? I pulled three planning datasets — the near-term 2025–2027 Programmed Cycling Projects, the 2022–2024 program just completed, and the original 10-Year Cycling Network Plan from 2016 — kept only committed protected facilities (cycle tracks, bike lanes, multi-use trails, bridges; dropped "Study or Design", "Signed and marked route", "Quiet Street"), and measured how much of each gap route the union of all plans covers.

The plans barely touch the gaps. Of the top 50 gap routes, only 5 have ≥ 30% coverage across built and planned infrastructure combined — and 45 are "orphan" gaps, with less than 15% combined coverage. Adding every committed plan on top of everything that exists today lifts average coverage on these routes from 12% to just 23%. The worst offender is Windsor/Newcastle ↔ Grand Avenue Park in Mimico: 3,229 trips a year, 0% built, 0% planned.

Magenta arcs are "orphan" gaps (high-demand, not built, not planned). Red arcs are gaps covered ≥ 30% by current plans. Green lines: built protected infrastructure. Amber lines: planned infrastructure (all committed plans unioned).

4. Bonus: station character

Bike-share rhythms reveal what kind of place each station is — residential (morning outflow, evening return), workplace (morning arrivals), weekend leisure, nightlife, or balanced hub. The clearest examples: Polson Pier is the city's strongest nightlife station (46% of weekday trips happen after 8pm, thanks to the nightclubs on the pier); King / Bay in the Financial District is the deepest workplace (commuter score −66%); the Brick Works sees 3× more bike traffic on weekends than weekdays.

Method. 2025 trip-level data pulled from the City of Toronto Open Data Portal. Station coordinates from the GBFS feed at bikesharetoronto.com. Protected bike-lane geometry from the City's Cycling Network dataset (GeoJSON, EPSG:4326, March 2026). OD aggregation and gravity fit in DuckDB + pandas; spatial overlay in GeoPandas (EPSG:2952, 50m buffer).

Caveats. Straight-line A→B is a proxy for the real ride path; a routing engine would give a stricter lane-coverage measure. The gravity baseline uses dock capacity as mass; it under-predicts trips to parks and transit hubs, so some "leisure" destinations show inflated affinity. Findings are descriptive — they identify where to look for missing infrastructure, not a causal claim about where bike lanes would most help.

Data gotcha worth documenting. The End_Station_Name column in the ridership CSVs is unreliable — it frequently echoes Start_Station_Name instead of the actual destination. All analyses here join by End_Station_Id against the GBFS station feed to recover true coordinates and names.
